CASE.01 · client 2020 – 2024 · Mumbai

Company A — Case Study

Marketing & Business Development · B2B technology reseller, Microsoft partner

During the pandemic, a mid-sized customer came to us wanting to buy Zoom licences and Google Drive cloud storage. He was the CEO, managing IT himself because the company wasn't big enough to justify a dedicated IT officer. Our technical team could see immediately that he didn't need any of it, but the conversation kept stalling because the jargon wasn't landing and he was getting frustrated, so eventually they assigned him to me.

My background isn't technical, and in this case that turned out to be the asset. I sat with him and explained, in plain language, that his existing Microsoft Enterprise licence already included pretty much everything he was about to pay twice for. He had Teams, he had OneDrive, he just hadn't looked at what his package covered because nobody had walked him through it in a way that didn't feel like being talked down to.

We ended up helping him set up Teams and OneDrive across his team, at no additional software cost to him.

The takeaway I've carried from this is that technical jargon can be a wall, and customers who feel overwhelmed tend to shut down, and once they've shut down even good advice stops landing. Meeting someone where they are, in language they actually use, isn't a soft skill sitting next to the real work. A lot of the time it is the real work. The deal we didn't close that day turned into a customer who trusted us on bigger things later.

ACAD.01 · thesis 2026 · solo

Thesis — AI marketing in privacy-conscious B2B

MA Strategic Marketing Management · ISM Berlin · 2026

A qualitative study of how German B2B firms actually evaluate AI-powered marketing tools (predictive lead scoring, automated LinkedIn outreach, the full pitch) in a market where data privacy and institutional trust change every adoption decision.

Twelve expert interviews across marketing, sales, IT, and procurement. The core finding was that adoption isn't a yes/no decision, it's a five-layer filter: infrastructure fit, relational appropriateness, governance alignment, regulatory conformity, and reputational credibility. AI tools stall because they fail at one of these checkpoints, not because they lack capability. I built this into a framework I'm calling the Negotiated Institutional Adoption Framework (NIAF).

The practical upshot is that firms accept AI when it augments analytical work inside their CRM, and reject it pretty hard the moment it tries to execute anything client-facing. Regulation turns out to be doing double duty here, because it slows adoption down but it also signals trust, which is an interesting tension. Vendors selling into Germany should lead with compliance, prioritise integration, and partner with incumbents rather than trying to disrupt them.

ACAD.02 · competition 2026 · group of 3

L'Oréal Brandstorm — [ intentionally blank ]

Case competition entry · 2026

A modular fragrance composition system designed for Maison Margiela Fragrances under L'Oréal Luxe. The starting question was simple: what would fragrance look like if Margiela's house philosophy of unfinished identity was taken seriously instead of treated as an aesthetic reference? We proposed a permanent, refillable object that lets you stack scent accords like building blocks and compose a new fragrance every day from your own collection.

The product is a matte black aluminium tower. Each half-block holds one curated accord inside a lifetime casing, and you stack them on a Bluetooth rod to build your collection. Twenty blocks yield around 1,140 three-accord compositions. An archive app reads each block passively through NFC and remembers every scent you've ever made, private by default, stored locally. The idea is that some compositions you can reproduce and some you can't, which basically mirrors how memory actually works.

The system also delivers on L'Oréal's 2030 sustainability targets. Where Margiela's existing REPLICA refill cuts packaging by 32%, ours gets closer to 80-90% because the casing never leaves circulation, and we built out the pricing architecture, unit economics, three-stream revenue model, persona work, and a DTC refill logistics model to back it up. The wider bet was that this could become brand-agnostic infrastructure across L'Oréal Luxe's 27 brands rather than just a Margiela product.

ACAD.03 · consulting 2025 · group of 5

Consulting Project — B2B Enterprise Technology Client

Group project · MA Strategic Marketing Management · ISM Berlin

A consulting engagement for a German B2B enterprise technology firm that wanted to reposition itself as a strategic technology partner for DACH-region clients with 1,000+ employees. My team and I delivered two linked pieces of work: a full marketing and communications strategy, and an employer branding strategy for the client's satellite employees.

On the marketing side, I worked on target market analysis and competitive benchmarking. We mapped what large enterprises actually need from a media tech partner (integration, reliability, compliance, transparent pricing) and where incumbents were falling short, and from there we built a go-to-market plan covering ATL channels like DOOH and business press, plus BTL tactics including whitepapers, LinkedIn ABM, enterprise dinners, and sponsored podcasts. The plan came with a €52K budget and a KPI framework spanning brand awareness, lead generation, revenue impact, and long-term trust.

On the employer branding side, we tackled the satellite employee problem, which is basically how to build belonging when half your workforce sits away from HQ. We proposed a tooling stack (Leapsome, Viva Engage, Coffeepals) tied to rituals that scale across locations, measured through pulse surveys and engagement KPIs.

ACAD.04 · teardown 2025 · group project

Spotify CRM Teardown

Group project · MA Strategic Marketing Management · ISM Berlin

A deep-dive into how Spotify uses CRM as a strategic engine rather than a customer support tool. My team and I mapped Spotify's full customer lifecycle across four lenses: how they acquire users (partnerships, freemium, virality), how they build relationships through data (the part I owned), how to calculate customer lifetime value across premium vs. ad-supported tiers, and where generative AI could extend the CRM stack.

My section focused on hyper-personalisation, specifically how Spotify combines collaborative filtering (what similar users listened to) with content-based filtering (audio signal analysis of danceability, tempo, key) to produce outputs like Discover Weekly, Daily Mix, and Spotify Wrapped. I also analysed their Graph Neural Network work on audiobook recommendations, which is a pretty concrete example of how CRM data becomes competitive advantage when a platform expands into adjacent content categories.

The core takeaway for me was that Spotify Wrapped isn't really a marketing stunt, it's user-facing CRM, turning invisible data collection into an emotional reason for users to stay subscribed.
